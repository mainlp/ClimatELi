[Climate change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change)
In [common usage](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Usage_(language)), [climate change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) describes [global warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change)—the ongoing increase in [global average temperature](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_temperature_record)—and [its effects](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change) on [Earth 's climate system](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_system).
[Climate change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) in a broader sense also includes [previous long-term changes to Earth 's climate](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change).
The current rise in [global average temperature](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_temperature_record) is more rapid than [previous changes](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change), and is primarily caused by [humans](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human) burning [fossil fuels](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel).
[Fossil fuel](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel) use, [deforestation](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation), and some [agricultural](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture) and [industrial](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industry_(economics)) practices increase [greenhouse gases](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas), notably [carbon dioxide](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide) and [methane](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane).
[Greenhouse gases](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas) [absorb](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)) some of the [heat](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat) that [the Earth](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth) [radiates](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation) after it warms from [sunlight](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunlight).
Larger amounts of these [gases](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas) trap more [heat](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat) in [Earth 's](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth) [lower atmosphere](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth), causing [global warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change).
Due to [climate change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change), [deserts](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desert) are expanding, while [heat waves](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_wave) and [wildfires](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildfire) are becoming more common.
Increased [warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) in [the Arctic](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic) has contributed to [melting](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting) [permafrost](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permafrost), [glacial retreat](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850) and [sea ice loss](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice_decline).
Higher [temperatures](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature) are also causing more [intense](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fujita_scale) [storms](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Storm), [droughts](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drought), and other [weather extremes](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme_weather).
Rapid [environmental change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) in [mountains](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain), [coral reefs](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coral_reef), and [the Arctic](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic) is forcing many [species](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species) to relocate or become [extinct](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction).
Even if efforts to minimise [future](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Future) [warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) are [successful](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Success), some [effects](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change) will continue for [centuries](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Century).
These include [ocean heating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean_heat_content), [ocean acidification](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean_acidification) and [sea level rise](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_level_rise).
[Climate change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) [threatens people](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_human_health) with increased [flooding](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flood), [extreme heat](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_wave), increased [food](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_security) and [water scarcity](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_scarcity), more [disease](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disease), and [economic loss](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_impacts_of_climate_change).
[Human migration](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_migration) and conflict can also be a result.
[The World Health Organization (WHO)](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization) calls [climate change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) the greatest [threat](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat) to [global health](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_health) in the [21st century](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/21st_century).
[Societies](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Society) and [ecosystems](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem) will experience more severe [risks](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk) without action to [limit warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation).
[Adapting to climate change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_adaptation) through efforts like [flood control](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flood_control) [measures](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement) or [drought-resistant crops](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xerophyte) partially reduces [climate change risks](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_risk), although some limits to [adaptation](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_adaptation) have already been reached.
Poorer [countries](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Country) are responsible for a small share of global [emissions](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions), yet have the least ability to [adapt](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_adaptation) and are most [vulnerable to climate change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_vulnerability).
[Many](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plural) [climate change impacts](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change) are already felt at the current 1.2 [°C](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius) (2.2 [°F](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fahrenheit) ) level of [warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change).
Additional [warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) will increase [these impacts](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change) and can trigger [tipping points](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tipping_points_in_the_climate_system), such as the [melting](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting) of the [Greenland ice sheet](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet).
Under the [2015 Paris Agreement](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Agreement), [nations](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Country) collectively agreed to keep [warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) "well under 2 [°C](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius)".
However, with pledges made under the [Agreement](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Agreement), [global warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) would still reach about 2.7 [°C](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius) (4.9 [°F](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fahrenheit) ) by the end of [the century](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/21st_century).
Limiting [warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) to 1.5 [°C](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius) will require halving [emissions](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas) by 2030 and achieving [net-zero emissions](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_zero_emissions) by 2050.
Reducing [emissions](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas) requires [generating electricity](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity_generation) from [low-carbon sources](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-carbon_electricity) rather than burning [fossil fuels](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel).
This change includes [phasing out coal and natural gas fired power plants](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel_phase-out), vastly increasing use of [wind](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wind_power), [solar](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_energy), [nuclear](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power) and other types of [renewable energy](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renewable_energy), and [reducing energy use](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Efficient_energy_use).
[Electricity generated](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity_generation) from [non-carbon-emitting sources](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_zero_emissions) will [need](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Need) to replace [fossil fuels](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel) for powering [transportation](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transport), [heating](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat) [buildings](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Building), and operating industrial facilities.
[Carbon](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon) can also be [removed](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal) from [the atmosphere](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth), for instance by increasing [forest cover](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_cover) and [farming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture) with [methods](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_method) that [capture carbon](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage) in [soil](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil).
[Terminology](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminology)
Before the [1980s](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1980s), when it was unclear whether the [warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) effect of increased [greenhouse gases](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas) were stronger than the [cooling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_transfer) effect of [airborne particulates](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particulates) in [air pollution](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_pollution), [scientists](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientist) used [the term](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminology) inadvertent [climate](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate) modification to [refer](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reference) to [human impacts on the climate](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment).
In the [1980s](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1980s), the [terms](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminology) [global warming and climate change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) became more common.
Though the two [terms](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminology) are sometimes used interchangeably, scientifically, [global warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) [refers](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reference) only to increased surface [warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change), while [climate change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) describes the [totality of changes to Earth 's climate system](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change).
[Global warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) — used as early as [1975](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1975)—became the more popular term after [NASA](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA) [climate scientist](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climatology) [James Hansen](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Hansen) used it in his [1988](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1988) [testimony](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Testimony) in the [U.S. Senate](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Senate).
[Since the 2000s](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/21st_century), [climate change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) has increased in usage.
[Climate change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) can also [refer](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reference) more broadly to both [human-caused changes](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change) or [natural changes](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change) throughout [Earth 's history](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Earth).
Various [scientists](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientist), [politicians](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politician) and [media](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_media) [now](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Present) use the [terms](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminology) [climate crisis](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_crisis) or [climate emergency](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_crisis) to talk about [climate change](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change), and [global heating](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) instead of [global warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change).
Observed [temperature rise](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change)
Multiple independent [instrumental datasets](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record) show that the [climate system](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_system) is [warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change).
The [2011 – 2020 decade](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010s) warmed to an average 1.09 [°C](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius) (0.95 – 1.20 [°C](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius) ) compared to the pre-industrial baseline (1850 – 1900).
[Surface temperatures](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_surface_temperature) are rising by about 0.2 [°C](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius) per [decade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decade), with [2020](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020) reaching a [temperature](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature) of 1.2 [°C](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius) above the pre-industrial era.
Since 1950, the [number](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) of [cold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold) [days](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Day) and [nights](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night) has decreased, and the [number](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) of [warm](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature) [days](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Day) and [nights](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night) has increased.
There was little net [warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) between the [18th century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/18th_century) and the mid - [19th century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/19th_century).
[Climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate) [information](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information) for that period comes from [climate proxies](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxy_(climate)), such as [trees](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree) and [ice cores](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_core).
[Thermometer records](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_temperature_record) began to provide global coverage around [1850](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1850).
[Historical](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History) [patterns](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pattern) of [warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) and [cooling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_transfer), like the [Medieval Warm Period](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_Warm_Period) and the [Little Ice Age](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Little_Ice_Age), did not occur at the same time across [different](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Difference_(philosophy)) regions.
[Temperatures](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature) may have reached as high as those of the late-[20th century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/20th_century) in a limited [set](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory) of regions.
There have been [prehistorical](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prehistory) [episodes](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Episode) of [global warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change), such as the [Paleocene – Eocene Thermal Maximum](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleocene%e2%80%93Eocene_Thermal_Maximum).
However, the modern observed [rise in temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) and [CO2](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide) [concentrations](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concentration) has been so rapid that even [abrupt geophysical events](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change) in [Earth 's history](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Earth) do not approach current rates.
[Evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evidence) of [warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) from air [temperature measurements](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature_measurement) are reinforced with a wide range of other [observations](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observation).
For example, [changes](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change) to the [natural water cycle](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_cycle) have been predicted and observed, such as an increase in the [frequency](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency) and intensity of heavy [precipitation](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitation), [melting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting) of [snow](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snow) and land [ice](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice), and increased [atmospheric humidity](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humidity).
[Flora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flora) and [fauna](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fauna) are also behaving in a manner consistent with [warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change); for instance, [plants](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant) are [flowering](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flower) earlier in [spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring_(season)).
Another key indicator is the [cooling](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_transfer) of the [upper atmosphere](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth), which demonstrates that [greenhouse gases](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas) are trapping [heat](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat) near the [Earth](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth) 's surface and preventing it from [radiating](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation) into [space](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space).
Regions of [the world](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth) [warm](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature) at differing rates.
The [pattern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pattern) is independent of where [greenhouse gases](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas) are [emitted](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions), because the [gases](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas) persist long enough to [diffuse](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion) across [the planet](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth).
Since the pre-industrial [period](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time), the [average surface temperature](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record) over land regions has increased almost twice as fast as the [global-average surface temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_temperature_record).
This is because of the larger [heat capacity](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_capacity) of [oceans](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean), and because [oceans](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean) lose more [heat](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_energy) by [evaporation](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evaporation).
The [thermal energy](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_energy) in the [global climate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_system) has grown with only brief pauses since at least [1970](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1970), and over 90[%](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percentage) of this extra [energy](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) has been [stored in the ocean](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean_heat_content).
The rest has [heated](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat) [the atmosphere](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth), [melted](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting) [ice](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice), and warmed the [continents](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent).
The [Northern Hemisphere](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere) and [the North Pole](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Pole) have warmed much faster than the [South Pole](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Pole) and [Southern Hemisphere](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere).
The [Northern Hemisphere](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere) not only has much more [land](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land), but also more [seasonal](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Season) [snow](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snow) cover and [sea ice](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_ice).
As these [surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface) flip from [reflecting](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_(physics)) a lot of [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light) to being [dark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darkness) after the [ice](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_ice) has [melted](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting), they start [absorbing](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)) more [heat](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat).
Local [black carbon](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_carbon) deposits on [snow](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snow) and [ice](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_ice) also contribute to [Arctic warming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_in_the_Arctic).
[Arctic temperatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_of_the_Arctic) are increasing at over twice the rate of the rest of the world.
[Melting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting) of [glaciers](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glacier) and [ice sheets](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_sheet) in [the Arctic](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic) disrupts [ocean circulation](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean_current), including a weakened [Gulf Stream](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Stream), further changing the [climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate).
